## Analysis

### Summary
Dylan interviews Dr. Wendy Charles, Chief Scientific Officer at BurstIQ, as part of the Women in Blockchain series. The conversation explores how blockchain technology is transforming healthcare data management, regulatory compliance (HIPAA, FDA), telehealth's post-COVID evolution, and how BurstIQ's LifeGraph blockchain enables longitudinal patient profiles while maintaining privacy through granular consent contracts.

### Topics Covered
- Dr. Charles's 30+ year career in clinical research, compliance, and health information technology
- HIPAA evolution from 1996 to present: privacy, security, and patient rights over health data
- Digital health technologies: wearables, remote sensors, GPS tracking for clinically meaningful endpoints
- Blockchain consent contracts: granular, dynamic consent for patient data sharing
- Telehealth post-COVID: insurance and licensing changes, limitations for physical exams
- FDA-led blockchain pilots for Drug Supply Chain Security Act compliance
- BurstIQ's LifeGraph: longitudinal blockchain profiles linking multiple data sets
- EHT Advisors partnership: supporting justice-involved individuals with linked services
- Estonia's blockchain layer on top of their healthcare system (since 2016)
- Personal health records: patient-managed, blockchain-enabled data portability
- Hidden hot wallets: abstracting blockchain complexity (private/public keys) behind usernames/passwords
- State of Wyoming and Colorado as blockchain-forward jurisdictions
- Monetization of health data: tokens, fiat currency, company shares
- Women in blockchain: growing representation as the industry expands beyond crypto

### Dylan's Takes & Opinions
- Dylan predicts that "by the time blockchain is mainstream, people will not even know they're using it" — comparing it to TCP/IP.
- He draws from personal experience with the US healthcare system after a bike accident to discuss his evolving opinions on healthcare.
- He references his graduate work in urban planning studying Housing First programs for chronically homeless individuals.
- Dylan notes the lack of communication between Colorado's 63 counties on shared databases as a real-world problem blockchain could solve.

### Guest Perspectives
**Dr. Wendy Charles (CSO, BurstIQ):** Dr. Charles positions blockchain as a necessary evolution in health information technology, not a replacement for existing systems. She argues that blockchain's inherent audit trail and smart contract automation make it 3x faster and cheaper than hard-coded alternatives. She emphasizes that patients should have more control over their data through dynamic consent contracts and that blockchain can enable more ethical approaches to data sharing. She sees the FDA's active participation in blockchain pilots as a strong signal for conservative institutions to adopt.
