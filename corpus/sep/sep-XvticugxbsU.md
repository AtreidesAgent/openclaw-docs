## Analysis

### Summary
Ganesh Swami, CEO and co-founder of Covalent, discusses how decentralized infrastructure can support the emerging AI economy, the critical importance of reading (not just writing) blockchain data, and the Ethereum Wayback Machine — a solution for preserving Ethereum's historical data as the network sheds it for scalability. The conversation covers blockchain's role in establishing truth and provenance in an AI-saturated world.

### Topics Covered
- Three facets of decentralized AI: training, inference, and data
- Inference as 95% of future AI compute spend
- Data provenance as the overlooked foundation for AI trust
- Ganesh's accidental entry into crypto from a physics/pharmaceuticals background
- Jeff Bezos's "focus on what doesn't change" philosophy applied to blockchain data
- The evolution of blockchain data needs from ICO era to present
- Reading vs writing blockchain data — an underserved problem
- Ethereum's modular vs monolithic architecture debate
- The Ethereum Wayback Machine: preserving historical data post-Dencun upgrade
- Blobs, data availability sampling, and 18-day deletion windows
- Short-term DA vs long-term DA (concept from EigenLayer's Sreeram)
- 4,000 testnet signups for Wayback Machine light clients
- Reasons for optimism: crypto adoption as a bipartisan issue

### Dylan's Takes & Opinions
- Expressed concern as a content creator about AI scraping podcasts to clone his voice and create unauthorized episodes — sees blockchain-based provenance as the solution.
- Noted that the longer you're in crypto, the easier it is to stop watching daily Bitcoin prices.
- Acknowledged that running an archive node requires significant technical expertise and is not accessible to average users.

### Guest Perspectives
**Ganesh Swami** argued that blockchain's promise is unfulfilled if you can't read from it — a fundamental gap most projects ignore while trumpeting write throughput. He drew a clear distinction between blockchains as "billboards" (post data, wait for challenge, move on) vs databases, explaining why historical data deletion is acceptable but requires a long-term preservation solution. He predicted that society will eventually demand to know the provenance of AI training data, creating "champagne vs sparkling wine" distinctions between decentralized and closed-source data. He expressed optimism that crypto's next 5 years will be more exciting than the last 5.

### People & Projects Mentioned
- **Covalent** — data infrastructure provider building the Ethereum Wayback Machine
- **Jeff Bezos / Amazon** — quoted: "focus on things that do not change"
- **Nvidia** — referenced for their view on training (5%) vs inference (95%) compute split
- **Meta** — credited for open-sourcing foundation models
- **OpenAI** — criticized for using data without compensation; Scarlett Johansson voice cloning incident
- **Sreeram Kannan / EigenLayer** — credited with short-term DA vs long-term DA concept
- **Ethereum / Dencun upgrade** — introduced blobs that delete after 18 days
- **Celestia, Avail DA** — other DA providers that also delete historical data
- **Solana, Base** — referenced in context of L2 ecosystem growth (Base TVL flipping Arbitrum)
- **Filecoin** — mentioned alongside other deepin projects showing real traction

### Recurring Themes
- Data as the overlooked pillar of the AI-blockchain intersection
- Truth and provenance as blockchain's most compelling value proposition
- Reading vs writing blockchain data as an underserved infrastructure need
- The tension between scalability (data deletion) and historical preservation
- Better, faster, cheaper as the timeless user demand

### Content Ideas & Future Topics
